Despite their “shy” reactions and slow movements, turtles are extremely important to their environments. They do so much more than just be part of the food chain. Some species help propagate various species of plants. While others shape the environment, often to the benefit of other plants and animals.

Unfortunately, despite their appearance suggesting of hardiness and endurance, turtles are deceptively fragile. That defensive shell, while useful against other would be predators does not do much against humanity’s technologies, such as lawn mowers, cars, and boats. Humans changing the environment also does not help either, such as introducing predators like cats and dogs, cutting down forests and polluting ponds and streams. We as a species do need to consider to be mindful of our actions, especially to those are much more beneficial. Plus, life is more fun with the existence of turtles.

Turtles as predators can help keep populations under-control, such as snails from overpopulating and then overeating marshlands or from sponges in the oceans from out competing against coral reefs. (Lovich, Jeffrey E; Ennen, Joshua R 2018) Some species of turtles are scavengers even, eating dead animals before they pollute the environment and bring on bacteria and diseases.

Herbivorous and omnivorous turtles can have similar effect with plant life. Keeping certain species in check, so that they do not overpopulate and compete with each other for resources. Some eat various berries and other seed bearing plants. These seeds will then be inevitably left behind in the turtles’ droppings, spreading out new plants across their environment. Some seeds have also been show to germinate far better after being digested by turtles. (Lovich, Jeffrey E; Ennen, Joshua R 2018)

Some species are excellent diggers. This digging helps the ecosystem by moving nutrients and resources around closer the surface from the underground, as well introducing additional resources to the soil, such animal litter, plant litter, water, and air. Not only that, but some like the gopher turtles make tunnels underground. These tunnels are not only a home for the tortoise, but for other animals as well, such as spiders, snakes, frogs, mice, rabbits, and many more. Due to having such a strong impact for other species, the gopher tortoise was given the title of “keystone species”, making it very important to protect. These tortoises may not been under too much pressure in their own ecosystem, but without them other species may suffer. (Lovich, Jeffrey E; Ennen, Joshua R 2018)

Turtle species have also been shown to actually restore environments too. Reintroduction efforts in the Galápagos have managed to not only curb woody plants from out-competing other species as well as helping certain plants propagate and spread. Eggs and hatchlings from sea turtles help feel other species as well as introducing more nutrients to the soils. (Lovich, Jeffrey E; Ennen, Joshua R 2018)

Kentucky turtles may not be as grand as some of the species mentioned. However, they still do provide for Kentucky’s ecosystem. They are not to be underestimated in usefulness.
